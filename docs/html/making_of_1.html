The Making Of Part 1 · the legend of Sneekie

Every other page on this site is engineering. This one is mythology. What follows is the story of Sneekie as I remember it — and after thirty-eight years, a few careers, and a generous quantity of beer, I remember it about as reliably as a goldfish remembers last Tuesday. I have no memory of this place. We’re going in anyway.

Narrator’s disclaimer. Dates are approximate. Details are reconstructed. Some of this may have happened to someone else entirely. I have decided to believe all of it.
I · 1988

A beige box and a warm summer

The legend begins in 1988, in front of a humming machine the colour of weak coffee. I was young, the room was too warm, and the only light came from a green phosphor glow and the blinking Ok of GW-BASIC.

HerbySoft — my software empire, staffed entirely by me — set out to make a game. There was no engine, no framework, no Stack Overflow to copy from. To draw something on the screen you POKEd character codes straight into video memory and hoped. I did a great deal of hoping.

II · the snake

Why a snake? I genuinely don’t know

Of all the things to spend a summer on, I built a snake in a maze. It ate hearts and clubs, shoved stones around, and fled little arrows that wandered the corridors with what I can only describe as malice. Then I did it again, thirty-two times, each level meaner than the last.

It was dangerous to go alone, so I gave the player a fistful of hearts and the snake a personality it absolutely did not need.

III · October 1988

It went to print

Here’s the part even I find suspicious: Sneekie was published. Printed. In MS(X)DOS Computer Magazine no. 25, October 1988 — page after page of my BASIC set in tiny type, so a total stranger could type the whole thing back in, line by line, semicolon by semicolon.

People actually did this. In 1988, that was the App Store.

I’d love to tell you how it felt to see my name in print. Honestly? I remember it the way you remember a dream that someone else described to you over the phone.
IV · 1988–2026

The lost years

Then time did what time does. Floppies rot. Drives vanish into drawers, and drawers vanish into house moves. The original disk — the actual source — is gone. Press F.

For decades the only surviving copy of Sneekie was ink on paper, in a magazine that almost nobody kept. The game existed the way a folk song exists: only because someone once wrote it down, somewhere, by hand. This is fine, I told myself. This is fine.

V · OCR, 2026

Reading it back from the dead

To revive it, we first had to read it — feed a scan of the printed listing to OCR, the technology that confidently turns pictures of letters back into letters. Mostly correct. It’s the “mostly” that ruins your week.

In a BASIC listing, 0 and O are identical twins separated at birth, and OCR cannot tell the zero from the oh. Nor 1 from l from I, nor 8 from B, nor 5 from S. One does not simply OCR a 1988 listing. Every wrong guess is a syntax error lying in ambush — and a single O where a 0 belongs is enough to stop the snake from ever being born.

VI · June 2026

Fable lit the spark, Opus built the house

And now the genuinely dramatic part. The line-for-line port — from 1988 BASIC to a game that runs in your browser — was done by Claude Fable, a model that was only available for a handful of days. A blink. It read the recovered listing and rebuilt Sneekie move for move, faithfully, while the window it existed in was already closing.

Then Claude Opus built everything around that port: the pages you’re reading, the chrome, the words, this very story. A relay race across thirty-eight years — a teenager with a POKE, a model that barely existed, and a model that turned it all into a website. The torch, passed in the dark.

The strange truth. The oldest part of this project is the only part that’s really mine, and it’s the part I remember least. The newest part was built in days, by machines I’d known for an afternoon.
