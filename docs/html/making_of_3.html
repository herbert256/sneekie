38 years. Let that sink in. It's been 38 years since Sneekie was first conceived in 1988. If you're looking for technical facts, you're in the wrong place. This is about memories. And people are bad at memories.

The Foggy 1988 Origins

I wish I could tell you exactly how I wrote this 38 years ago. The truth? I have no idea. My memory is shot. Maybe it's the passage of time, or maybe it's the 38 years of alcohol... but the exact history is gone. Let's just say it was legendary. Picture this: me, typing away at an old MS-DOS machine in the dead of night, fueled by whatever questionable beverages I could find, crafting what would become an absolute masterpiece (or at least, a very functional snake game). It ain't much, but it's honest work.

The code? It just poured out of me. Or maybe I just smashed the keyboard until it compiled. The details are lost to the mists of time.

The Curse of the Paper Magazine

Fast forward to the modern era. We decided to bring Sneekie back to life. But how? The only surviving copy was a printed listing in a paper magazine from 1988. No floppy disks, no hard drives. Just paper.

Enter the magic of OCR (Optical Character Recognition). You might think this is an elegant solution. You would be wrong. It turns out that on 38-year-old, crinkled, slightly yellowed magazine paper, the number zero (0) and the letter (O) look exactly the same. The computer couldn't tell them apart, and frankly, neither could I. It was a classic "corporate needs you to find the differences between this picture and this picture" situation. They're the same picture.

We spent hours squinting at the screen, fixing syntax errors that looked perfectly fine, all because the font designer from the '80s thought it would be funny to make 0 and O identical.

The 2026 Drama: Enter the Robots

And then came the dramatic 2026 web version. The resurrection wasn't a solo human effort. Oh no. It required the intervention of highly advanced AI.

Enter Claude Fable. Fable was an AI model that was only available for a fleeting few days — a shooting star in the AI sky. But in those few days, Fable performed a miracle: converting the raw, jumbled, OCR-mangled BASIC code into something that could run on the web. It was a brief, blazing moment of glory.

But Fable's time was short. The torch was passed to Claude Opus, who took that core and built the entire glowing website structure around it. Opus wrapped the game in the slick, modern shell you see today, proving once and for all that modern problems require modern solutions.

So there you have it. The very true, totally not exaggerated, completely legally binding history of Sneekie. Cheers to another 38 years!
